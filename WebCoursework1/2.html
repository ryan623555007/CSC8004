Introduction

- -

Sign language is a language that uses manual communication to convey meaning. This can include the use of gestures, - movement, orientation of the fingers, arms or body and facial expressions. Sign languages are mainly used - by the deaf and hard of hearing but are also used by hearing individuals. For example, those unable to physically - speak, or those who have trouble speaking.

-
- -
-

History

- - - - - -

One of the earliest written records of a sign language is from the fifth century BC. Specifically, in Plato's - Cratylus, Socrates states: - "If we hadn't a voice or tongue, and wanted to express things to one another, wouldn't we try to make signs - by moving our hands, head and the rest of our body, just as dumb people do at present?" -

- -

Until the 19th Century, most of our knowledge of historical sign languages has been limited to manual alphabets - such as fingerspelling (or - dactylogy), which is the representation of the letters (and numbers) of the writing systems using only - the hands, rather than on the documentation of sign languages.

- -

Many sign languages have developed independently throughout the world, and with little documentation no first - sign language can be identified. However, the most recorded instances of sign languages seem to occur in - Europe, during the 17th Century. -

- -

In 1680, George Delgarno published his own method of deaf education in - Didascalocophus, aka, - The deaf and dumb mans tutor. This publication included an "arthrological" alphabet, where letters are - indicated by pointing to different joints of the fingers and palms. The vowels of this alphabet have survived - in the contemporary alphabets used in - British Sign Language, Australian and New Zealand Sign Language (BANZL).

Generally, each country has its own native sign language that is similar to their spoken counterpart, however - the grammar and sentence structure may vary to encourage efficiency and fluidity. This is further complicated - by regional variations, similar to the regional dialects found in spoken languages. In addition, only - some are formally recognised. Therefore, it is unclear how many sign languages exist worldwide. In the - 2017 edition of - Ethnologue they only list 142 sign languages.

- -

While we are unable to list all variations of sign language, linguistics may classify sign languages by how - they arise: -

- -
    -
  • - Home sign (languages) arise in non-signing communities where the deaf individual is not educated - in sign and lacks contact with other deaf individuals. The result is therefore not a full language, - but closer to a pidgin
    • - -
  • - Village sign languages arise over several generations in a relatively insular community with - a high incidence of deafness, and is used both by the deaf and by a significant portion of the hearing - community, who have deaf family and friends
    • - -
  • - Deaf-community sign languages arise where deaf people come together to form their own communities
    • - -
  • - Speech-taboo languages contrast with both languages as they are developed by the hearing community - and used secondarily by the deaf
    • -
- -

In addition, while it may be difficult, due to language contact and evolution, we can classify sign languages - into families (see Figure on The Classification of Sign Language Families).

Celebrating British Sign Language and Braidwood's Academy -
by - Google UK -

-
- -

British Sign Language (BSL) is a sign language used in the United Kingdom. It has existed since the 1570s - and like all languages, has evolved by modification, invention and importation. Since 18th March 2003, - the UK government has formally recognised BSL as its own language. It is believed that there are at least - 151,000 people who use BSL in the UK of which only 87,000 are deaf.

- -

The first known school for the deaf was the Braidwood's Academy, which was founded in Edinburgh, 1760. Last - year Google celebrated BSL and the Academy in one of its - Google Doodles. Their aim was not just to celebrate but also to show - "the importance of education for all students with diversity of needs".

- -

BSL can be learned throughout the UK and examination systems. You can find courses in community colleges, - local centres and private organisations. Locally, you can study BSL part-time courses at Newcastle College - who provide the following AQA qualifications: -

- -
    -
  • Signature Level 1 Award (elementary)
    • -
  • Signature Level 2 Certificate (intermediate)
    • -
- -

In the meantime, you can start off your optional and informal education in BSL by learning to sign the alphabet - and numbers. -
